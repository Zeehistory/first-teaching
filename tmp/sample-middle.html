To repeat, I use square brackets most commonly in translations of the Qur’ān and Ḥadīth, which use common modes of ellipsis that are implicitly understood in the original Arabic text but cannot be easily detected in literal English translations. Yet, they are necessary or helpful in context for the proper understanding of the original passage.

It is standard procedure, when taking some words out of a quotation, to employ an ellipsis of three dots to indicate that one or more words have been taken out. I often avoid this convention, however, especially when quoting Qur’ānic verses and Ḥadīth. Thus, I may often leave out such ellipsis marks—especially at the beginning or end of a quotation—because I find them unesthetic, unnecessary, and occasionally distracting.

Numbers & Numerals

As regards numbering and numerals, all quantities are spelled out that are made up of one or two words, although I generally use numerals with them if they extend into the thousands, millions, or beyond. In such cases, the numerals are put in parentheses, directly after the spelled-out version. In these instances, numerals are added because they make reading the number easier. Spelling out numbers is, however, the book’s default position: as in, nine, one hundred, one thousand, fifty thousand, seven million, and so forth. In all such cases—even though the figure may be much larger than a thousand or a million—they are easily identified in spelled out form, and no particular benefit is provided by giving numerals. Numbers from one to one hundred, for example, are written out without giving their counterparts in Arabic numerals. But—as just indicated—Arabic numerals in parentheses may be used for larger enumerations after the spelled-out quantity: As in one hundred fifty-seven thousand, five hundred and fifty-six (157,556).

Assigning Historical Dates

This book consistently uses the dating systems of both the Islamic and the Common Era, whenever the two overlap. This rule makes it easy for the reader-listeners—especially those who are Muslims—to place both dates accurately in the historical chronologies of the Islamic and non-Islamic worlds. When dates do not coincide, the book uses BCE (before the Common Era) for pre-Common Era dates and CE (Common Era) for dates falling within the first six centuries of the early Christian period prior to the rise of Islam.

It is the Western custom to date the Common Era beginning with the birth of Jesus Christ. In reality, however, the exact year of the nativity of Jesus Son of Mary[40] is not certain. According to modern Scholars, Jesus was probably born either in the year 7 BCE, 6 BCE, or 4 BCE. Thus, the second solar-year millennium after the birth of Christ would actually have taken place sometime between the years 1413/1993 and 1416/1996, and not in 1420/2000, as is commonly presumed and when the dawn of the new millennium was so earnestly and often fearfully anticipated around the world.

The Christian West only began to date the Common Era from the time of the Christ’s birth during the sixth century CE, and that same dating system was only later accepted in the Christian East. Both customs, then, were adopted only a relatively short time before Muslims began to date their calendars from the time of the Prophet’s Hijra (Migration) from Mecca to Medina, which is the basis of the Islamic calendar. The Prophet Muḥammad’s Mission lasted about twenty-three lunar years, from thirteen years before the Hijra or 13 BH/610 until 11/632. Muslims call these pivotal years of universal Salvation History “the Age of Supreme Happiness” (‘Aṣr al-Sa‘āda). The Prophet Muḥammad was born on 22 April 571 CE, and his passing was at the age of sixty-three lunar years on Monday 12 Rabī’ al-Awwal 11/8 June 632. The Second Rightly Guided Caliph, ‘Umar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb,[41] whose Caliphate began in the year 12/633 and lasted until 24/645, established the Islamic lunar calendar based upon the Prophet’s Hijra. He believed that Muslims should have a calendar of their own, not just because Islam constituted a new religious Dispensation,[42] but also because it stood out as a new and revolutionary human Civilization and universal way of life. ‘Umar called the Companions of the Prophet together to give him counsel on the matter. Various Companions proposed basing the Muslim calendar on the Prophet’s birth, his death, or his reception of the first Qur’ānic Revelation. But the Caliph ‘Umar perceived that the seminal historical event in the Prophet’s Mission was his Hijra from Mecca to Medina, where Islam finally became free and was able to become the religious, socio-political, and cultural reality God had designed it to be. In The First Teaching, when the Islamic and Common Era overlap, the first century cited is the Islamic date according to Islam’s lunar calendar, while the second citation is to the Common Era (CE) solar date that overlaps with it.

Throughout the footnotes of this work, it has been my habit to provide the death dates and some words of introduction in the footnotes regarding important figures when they are first mentioned in the book. This can be seen, for example, in the notes when I cite Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (d. 899/1494), the noted Islamic Scholar al-Imām Muḥammad ibn Ḥamdūn (d. 1273/1857), or the Australian bishop and social activist Ernest H. Burgmann (d. 1387/1967). I do not provide birth dates for those who have passed away, because final dates are generally adequate for identifying the general period in which they flourished, while dates of birth are often more difficult to establish. In the case of contemporaries, however, I cite only their dates of birth, which is meant to indicate that they are still living. In all such cases, I write out the word “born,” since the abbreviation “b.” is easily mistaken for “d.”, which is used for dates of death. Thus, for example, I follow this method in presenting Sister Mary Prudence Allen (born 1359/1940), al-Sayyid al-Shaykh Quṣayy Abū-al-Si‘d al-Ḥusaynī al-Rifā‘ī al-Shafi‘ī (born 1386/1967), and his somewhat older contemporary Grand Āyat-Allāh al-Sayyid Kamāl al-Ḥusaynī al-Ḥaydarī (born 1375/1956).

Book Profile & Scope

Speaking the Truth with Love is the introductory volume of First Teaching of the Last Message: The Divine Science and Its Six Pillars. The work is meant to be a reliable, wide-ranging, and in-depth reference for the six foundational Pillars (Arkān) of Islam’s universal and timeless Prophetic Message. It also branches out under the canopy of those pillars to include many of the vast implications they affirm regarding the structure of reality in the world around us and our sound cognition of it. Given its scope, the compendium’s subject matter is designed to explain in the fullest possible sense the fathomless meanings—explicit and implicit—in Islam’s two-part Prophetic testimony of faith. All such ramifications of Islām—inwardly and outwardly—are included in its two-fold Creedal witness:

Lā ilāha ill-Allāh

There is no god but God

Muḥammadun Rasūlu-Llāh

Muḥammad is God’s Messenger

In Arabic, the word Islām[44] refers to the act of freely rendering submissive obedience to God.[45] But the obedient disposition of islām is not exclusive to Human Beings. Rather, islām is a distinctive hallmark in all of God’s creation—from the winding galaxies, stars, and planets above, to the fishes of the sea, the birds of the air, and all other plants and animals on earth as well as the microorganisms and even minerals and other inorganic substances we know as well as the atoms and molecules that make them up—all of which are muslim, glorifying God in their unique ways and existing in complete submission to His Infinite Knowledge, Perfect Wisdom, and Eternal Will.

As a historical Dispensation, Islam stands in direct continuity with all earlier Prophetic religion—whether pre-Biblical, Biblical, or extra-Biblical—and it must be emphasized in this regard that the imperative of volitional submission to the Divine, which lies at the core of islām, perfectly epitomizes the ancient concept of Prophetic Religion in all earlier Revelatory Traditions. Biblical Scripture asserts: “Let us hear the conclusion of the whole matter: Revere God and keep His Commandments. For this is the whole duty of Man” (Ecclesiastes, 12:13). Thus, islām—even in the Biblical worldview—constitutes “the conclusion of the whole matter,” which in Gospel Christianity calls to mind “That Old-Time Religion,” which Christian Protestants have long counted among their joyous Jubilee Songs.[46] In a similar vein, the Biblical Book of Proverbs stresses: “Where there is no [Revelatory] Vision, the people perish. But blessed be the one who heeds the instruction of [Sacred] Wisdom” (Proverbs, 29:18). This quest for the treasures of God’s primordial religion, authentic Revelatory Vision, and Sacred Wisdom runs like a golden thread through the entire weft of The First Teaching.

The six Buttresses of Prophetic credence as established by Islam’s two-part testimony of faith are belief in:

  1. God
  2. The Angels
  3. The Revealed Books
  4. The Prophet-Messengers
  5. The Resurrection, Last Judgment, & Hereafter
  6. Providence & the Foreordination[47] of all things, including Good and Evil.

The field of study that articulates these foundations, their full implications, and other corollaries of Islam’s two-part Prophetic declaration of faith is called both the Divine Science (al-‘Ilm al-Ilāhī) and the Highest Science (al-‘Ilm al-A‘lā).[48] As we will see in what follows, this thorough-going study in Islam of Divinity, Prophecy, the Seen, and the Unseen constitutes a universal Metaphysical and historical discipline, which may be defined as: “The Science[49] of what is as it [truly] is, according to the rubric of Islam (Al-‘Ilm bi-al-mawjūd bi-mā huwa mawjūd ‘alā qanūn al-Islām).” This explication of the Divine Science, which has been adopted in The First Teaching and is explained further in this and later volumes, is virtually identical to the Science of Metaphysics as defined by Aristotle[50] and Ibn Sīnā (Latin: Avicenna),[51] but with the all-important difference of establishing Islamic Revelation as the basic standard and ultimate criterion for all sound Metaphysical and speculative discourse about God and ultimate realities.

First and foremost, the Divine or Highest Science bears the burden of delineating and explaining the Creedal fundamentals of Orthodox Islamic belief. This primary function of Divinity in Creedal matters sets forth all obligatory Doctrines, which all Muslims are required to affirm. By its nature, Creedal belief in Islam is relatively straightforward, although its instructional demands vary among individuals according to their cultures, backgrounds, and personal needs. Such Creedal and Doctrinal obligations—as will be clarified in what follows—pertain exclusively to those tenets of the Prophetic teaching which—beyond any shade of doubt—are clearly and unambiguously articulated and established as literally true in Islam’s Revealed and Scholarly sources—the Qur’ān,[52] the Sunna,[53] and Consensus (al-Ijmā‘)[54]—in conformity with the exacting standards of semantic clarity laid down by the dictates of Islam’s Epistemology of Certainty[55] (Uṣūl al-Qaṭ‘ wa al-Ẓann).

Homō Religiōsus[57]—“Man[58] the Believer”—requires, as we will illustrate in Volume Ten, sound and meaningful answers to all his existentialist and religious queries and questionings, especially the chief issues that perplex him and preoccupy his consciousness from time to time. Book Title in Perspective

I called this book First Teaching of the Last Message to reflect the fact that, in Islam, seeking sound Creedal and Doctrinal foundations and getting reliable anchorage in Epistemology and Divinity constitute the first and foremost religious Individual Obligation (Farḍ ‘Ayn) for every Muslim of legal age and having basic moral competence (being “mukallaf”). Thus, such matters rightfully constitute “a first teaching” in terms of their pedagogical priority. But, as noted earlier, this individual duty is complemented and, in many ways, vastly superseded by the immense Communal Obligation (Farḍ Kifāya) that Muslims bear as a World Community to galvanize their system of Islamic belief in its fullest sense so that it may correctly and effectively address all of the critical questions of our time. Although not an Individual Obligation in itself, this broader dimension of the Divine Science is clearly a Communal Muslim duty of the first order. Thus, the subject matter of the book as a First Teaching seeks to deal with these first most fundamental priorities of Islam and their cultural amplifications at both the levels of Individual and Societal Obligations.

As such an initial teaching, the book’s title also implies that appropriate second, third, fourth, and other ordinal studies should follow. According to the dictates of Islamic pedagogy, a Second Teaching, for example, would focus upon the Prophetic Way of Life (al-Sharī‘a), concentrating on Individual Obligations (Furūḍ al-‘Ayn) primarily, while introducing and bringing to light all fundamental and secondary Communal Obligations (Furūḍ Kifāya) that are required—not of each individual Muslim—but of the Islamic World Community as a whole. While The First Teaching seeks to set forth the primary obligations of Muslims regarding Divinity and the fullest definition of reality as it truly is, The Second Teaching would seek to develop competence in such basic personal responsibilities and understandings that make up the ways of a righteous life that Believers seek to live both as individuals and Communities. Further third, fourth, and other teachings would include topics such as the principles of Morality[67] and Ethics,[68] good character, the Science of the purification of the heart, the elucidation of the Spiritual Path, Prophetic biography, the lives of the Companions, Islamic and world history, as well as much broader and more detailed empirical treatments of Occidentalism.

In Islam—which is the Prophetic Path—mastering the basic fundamentals of the Divine Science (al-‘Ilm al-Ilāhī) and then the personal parameters of the Law are not only first and second-order imperatives, they are also the essential first two steps for Islamic Spirituality. Thus, any attempts to follow an authentic and ultimately successful Path towards authentic experiential Knowledge of God (al-Ma‘rifa) cannot happen without sound Creedal foundations and adequate grounding in the Prophetic Code. Without them, no authentic inward journey is possible, even if—for an entire lifetime—the Believer has the best intentions, worships intensely, and seeks earnestly to discover himself and the secrets of sincere Spirituality.

The title’s reference to “the Last Message” reflects the Islamic belief in the finality of the Prophecy of the Prophet Muḥammad and the conviction that no Divine Messenger will come after him. This Doctrine is an essential part of the content of the Islamic Creed, which is affirmed by the Qur’an, Sunna, and the Scholarly Consensus (al-Ijmā‘) of the Community. In Islamic belief, the Prophet Muḥammad was the Prophet of the End of Days and the Seal of all God’s tens of thousands of Prophets and Messengers who came to Humanity during the millennia before him and prior to the return of Jesus Christ at the end of the world. The finality of the Prophet’s Messengerhood does not conflict with the Second Coming of the Messiah Jesus Son of the Virgin Mary—which is also an obligatory Islamic Creedal belief and is examined more fully in Volumes Two and Fifteen. For the Second Coming constitutes the return of a former Prophet-Messenger, Jesus Christ, not the sending of a new one. This fact is indicated by the adjective “second,” since Jesus—as is well known and universally acknowledged by those who believe in him—came a first and initial time over two thousand years ago. In Islamic belief, the Messiah Jesus Son of Mary will return at the end of the present world-order—not as a new Prophet or to establish an alternative religious Dispensation, but to correct the errors of the historical Church and Rabbinic Judaism, while vindicating the teaching of his Brother and Advocate, the Prophet Muḥammad, God’s Paraclete,[70] whose teaching authentically represents and effectively defends the Messiah’s own original Message prior to its historical distortion in later centuries. Upon his return, Jesus, the Messiah, will successfully champion the upright Muslims of that final generation in Humanity’s ultimate, victorious struggle against the Antichrist (al-Dajjāl).[71] All of these beliefs are fundamental Eschatological teachings. They are more fully discussed in Volume Seventeen and constitute obligatory articles of Islamic Creed and Doctrine.

Regarding Sources of Truth & Cognition

The First Teaching is fundamentally concerned with Truth, although it seeks to speak it consistently with love, as we will propose in what follows. In this context, it is useful to introduce how the Islamic Scholarly Tradition looked upon the sources of Truth and cognition as well as the acquisition, cultivation, and application of knowledge, especially since The First Teaching aspires to be a meaningful adjunct to that Tradition.

First and above all, Human knowledge belongs supremely and exclusively to God alone, and—as it is said—there is no patent on the sun or the moon. Just as God fashioned the sun, the moon, and the entire universe, it is He alone who creates and owns the ideas of Human Beings—and people “receive” and “acquire” them from Him.

Image 12:

Who can patent the sun and the moon?

In this process, God imparts knowledge and understanding to whomever He wills, and only He wills to allow thinkers to gather that knowledge together, synthesize, and comprehend it. God says in the Qur’ān: “And they encompass nothing of His knowledge but as He wills.” (The Cow, 2:255).

Thus, knowledge is always “Divinely created” and “Divinely given,” which—as we will see in our discussion of causation, Foreordination, and Free Will in Volume Eleven—is only “acquired” by Human beings under the auspices of their individual freedom to act. Thus, strictly speaking, Human knowledge is always a Divine Creation is never a personal or social cognitive construct. We are “given” what we know—however unique or great—and we do not “create” it. It begins and ends with God, not with the subjective human consciousness. At the same time, however, errors may be rightly attributed to individuals, although, those shortcomings and faults too are created by God and then “acquired” by the agency those very same Human Beings who make them. Great thinkers, however, are receptors of God-given ideas, not the inventors of them.

Moreover, Traditionally in the Islamic world, Muslims were grateful to Scholars who synthesized and expressed the Truth for them cogently, even when those learned authorities presented their material without citing their sources, which was a common custom in the Traditional Islamic World. For, in the pre-Modern past, Muslims did not entertain the illusion that any Human authors had actually “created” the knowledge they presented, much less that they “owned” it or, in Modern parlance, were justified in “copyrighting” it. Again, knowledge was seen as a Divine Light that is uncovered in the human heart as a gift directly from God to those who have the receptivity to learn and communicate it.

In the process of learning, writing, and teaching, new and distinct worlds present themselves to us, and there is an intrinsically isomorphic relation[74] between the cognitive apparatus God has given us as individuals and the universe of thoughts, concepts, and knowledge that they disclose. Thus, knowledge by its nature is highly reactive, interactive, and affective (infi’ālī), corresponding to the inner geography of each person’s heart and their Intellectual and Spiritual life. As a consequence, the reception and transmission of knowledge and wisdom are always a process of personal illumination in the attempt to bring light to others as well.
